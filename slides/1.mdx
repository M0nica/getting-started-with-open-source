# Different roles in Open Source

- **user** (read access only) - someone who passively consumes software
- **contributor** (_may_ have write access) - someone who has contributed to a piece of software
- **maintainer** (write access) - someone who is responsible for the long-term maintainance of a project and signing off on changes, they may be the original project creator or a user that turned into a contributor and then ultimately a maintainer.

<SpeakerNotes>
  A user passively consumes software. A contributor is someone who has
  contributed to improving a piece of software in any capacity, some
  contributors may more-so identify as user depending on how signficant their
  contributions are. A maintainer has the most responsibility and is responsible
  for the long-term maintainence of ap roject and signing off on any changes. A
  project creator can also dissolve themselves of the responsibility of being a
  maintainer. For very popular projects it is necessary to have a maintainer who
  can spend the majority of their time on project management and triage.
</SpeakerNotes>

# Some best practices when contributing to Open Source?

- Avoid submitting unsolicited changes.
- Contribute in areas that a maintainer has already given the green light OR open a discussion in a GitHub issue with suggested functionality.
- Just because a project is Open Source doesn't automatically mean that it's open to new contributors and you can usually get a sense of how supportive a community is of new contributors based on their documentation (or lackthereof)

<SpeakerNotes>
  The benefit of spending the time to find issues that are well-scoped to your
  interest and experience level is that if you make an attempt at resolving an
  issue based on the requirements usually maintainers are more willing to work
  with you to help get your changes accepted into a project.
</SpeakerNotes>
